This system was designed to give a logical and navigable order to a complex inventory, following the path of each product from its general conception to the specific item ready for sale. At the highest level, the entire catalog is organized into large **Categories**, which function like departments in a department store, such as "Clothing" or "Electronics". Each **Category**, identified by its own unique `Code` and described by a `Name`, groups together a wide range of goods. Within each of them, we find a more detailed classification: the **Product Type**. For example, in the "Clothing" category we might have the product types "Pants" and "Shirts". Each **Product Type**, with its specific `Code` and `Name`, belongs exclusively to a single large **Category**, thus creating a clear and easy-to-consult hierarchy.

Moving from the general concept to something more concrete, we encounter the **Model**, which represents a specific product line. A **Model** is uniquely defined by its `Variant` within a given **Product Type**, allowing you to distinguish, for example, a "Slim Fit Shirt" from a "Regular Fit Shirt". The structure also offers considerable flexibility, since one **Model** can be linked to another, creating families of products in which a basic model can have different declinations or subsequent versions. The beating heart of the inventory is the **Article**: if the **Model** is the project, the **Article** is the physical specimen found in the warehouse. Each **Item** is an instance of a **Model** and is uniquely identified by its `SKU` code within that model. Furthermore, each **Item** has a `Status` that describes its current condition, such as "available" or "sold", allowing timely management of stocks.

What gives commercial identity to each **Article** is the **Brand**. Every single item in the catalog is associated with a unique **Brand**, defined by its `ID` and `Name`. The brands themselves are placed in a broader context: a single **Brand** can be active in multiple **Categories** of products, for example a famous brand could produce both clothing and accessories. Finally, to complete the production chain, the system traces the origin of these brands. Each **Brand** is supplied by a single **Supplier**, registered with its own `ID` and its `Company Name`, guaranteeing a complete view not only of what is sold, but also of who produces it and who distributes it.
