Imagine having to map the complex architecture of an organization, a system designed to digitally represent its physical and hierarchical structure. At the center of this organizational universe we find the Headquarters, which represent the physical pillars of the company, such as the main branches or campuses. Each location is unique and unmistakable, identified by its own specific code. Within each of these locations, working life takes place in the offices. An Office cannot exist in the abstract; it is always physically located within one and only one location, creating an indissoluble bond. In fact, to identify an Office uniquely, its internal code is not enough, but it is also necessary to specify the Office to which it belongs. This ensures that each workspace is clearly contextualized, allowing the system to know that the "Administration" office of the Rome office is distinct from that of the Milan office.

To bring these spaces to life, people come into play. Assistants are the operational staff assigned to each Office. Each Assistant is exclusively associated with a single Office, creating a clear map of who works where. Although an Office can host numerous Assistants, each Assistant has a single reference workstation. Management, however, is designed with greater flexibility through the figure of the Manager. The relationship between managers and offices is dynamic and complex: a single manager may have the responsibility of supervising multiple offices, perhaps coordinating different functional areas. At the same time, a particularly strategic or complex office could be co-managed by multiple managers, each with specific skills. This flexible model allows you to represent modern managerial structures, where responsibilities can be shared and supervision can extend across multiple fronts, reflecting an agile and interconnected organization.
