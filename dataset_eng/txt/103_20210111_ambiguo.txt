In this system, we have a little bit of everything. There are shops, each with its own identification code, a name and an address where to find it. These shops are not all the same, but they fall into certain categories, which we call types, each with its own acronym and a name to clarify. The thing is flexible: a shop can be of multiple types at the same time and, of course, one type can be associated with a lot of different shops.

Then there is the staff, the employees, each registered with a serial number, name and surname. Their connection to the stores is a bit tangled in the sense that an employee might work in more than one store, and one store clearly has quite a few people working there. Speaking of employees, we also keep track of a couple of things related to cities, which are simply locations with a code and a name. For each employee, we know in which city he was born and, separately, in which city he currently resides; of course, multiple people may be born or live in the same place.

Finally, there are the products that are sold. Each product has its own code and name, but to identify it truly well you must also know who made it, i.e. the manufacturer. Each manufacturer, which in turn has a code and a name, can churn out a lot of different stuff, but a single product comes out of only one manufacturer. These products are also organized by type: each item belongs to one and only one type, while one type can contain an infinite number of different items.
