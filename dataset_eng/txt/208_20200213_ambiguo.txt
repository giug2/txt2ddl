So, basically the whole world of cinema is managed here. We have films, each with its own identification code, title and plot. Each film is linked to a single production company, which in turn has a name, a location and perhaps a top director, and obviously a production company can churn out a lot of films. These films are then shown in cinemas; each cinema has its own name and code, and can host a lot of screenings. A screening is a specific event, with a date and time, and only ever refers to a single film, even if that film may be shown a thousand times. Going into more detail, a film is made up of many scenes, each with its title and code. This is flexible, because a scene can theoretically appear in multiple films and a film is a montage of many different scenes, so you need to keep track of their order. The scenes, then, are written by a screenwriter, identified by a code and a name; a screenwriter can write an infinite number of scenes, but each scene is the work of just one person. Finally there are the actors, registered with a serial number, name and surname. To attach an actor to a film we use something we call "participation". In essence, this participation is a kind of contract that says that a certain actor, on behalf of a certain production company, is playing a specific role. The roles, such as "the hero" or "the comic sidekick", have their own code and name. Therefore, an actor can have many participations, a production company manages several of them, but each participation ties an actor and a production company to a single and precise role.
