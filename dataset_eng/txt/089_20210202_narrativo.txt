Imagine an information system designed to comprehensively track and manage public health in the area, starting from large geographical areas up to the individual citizen. It all starts from the largest administrative subdivision, the **Region**, each individual region is uniquely identified by an `IDReg` code and known through its `Name`. The healthcare organization of each Region is then divided into different **ASL** (Local Health Authorities), which represent the direct point of contact with the territory. Each ASL is in turn unique, defined by an `ASL Code` that distinguishes it from all the others, and has a specific `Name` and a physical `Address`. The link between these two levels is very clear: a Region can include and coordinate numerous ASLs, but each ASL belongs to and refers to one and only one Region.

Going into further detail, each local health authority has the responsibility of assisting a community of people, the **Patients**. In our system, each patient is registered with their `Name` and, to guarantee their unique and irrefutable identification, with their Fiscal Code (`CF`). Here too, the relationship is precise: a single ASL takes care of many patients, but each patient is registered and refers to a single ASL of competence.

The operational heart of this monitoring system is the management of diagnostic tests, the **Swabs**. A patient can undergo multiple swabs over time, and the system keeps track of each one. For each swab we record crucial information such as the `Date` of execution and the final `Result`. To ensure that each individual test is tracked without the possibility of error, its identification is not based on a single code, but on the unique combination of a specific test code, `CT`, and the identifier of the patient who carried it out. This means that each record of a swab is inextricably linked to the person who performed it, telling the health history of an individual through his or her tests: a patient may have a history of many swabs, but every single swab belongs exclusively to a single patient.
