So, here we have a system that manages everything related to theaters. There are the **Actors**, each with their own identification code, name and surname, nothing strange. Then there are the **Theatrical works**, that is, the actual texts, comedies, dramas, each with a code, a title and the year of release. These works, obviously, are written by the **Authors**; we keep track of them with a code, personal data and dates of birth and death. It seems that an author can write a lot of works, but each work is linked to only one author.

All this comes to life in **Theatres**, which are registered with a code, name, address and telephone number. A theater can put on a lot of different shows. To keep track of what's being performed where, there's something called **Opera on the Playbill**. It's a kind of card that links a specific play to a specific theater for a certain **Period**. Therefore, the same work can be shown in multiple theatres. In each play on the bill, obviously, various actors play, and an actor can very well be engaged in several shows at the same time.

To go and see one of these shows, you need **Tickets**. A ticket is strictly linked to a single work on the bill, so you know exactly for which show and in which theater it is valid. Each ticket has its own **Price** and also refers to a **Seat Type**, which is simply a category like "stall" or "gallery", each with its own code and description. Finally, there are the various discounts, called **Reduction Type**. Each theater can offer different types of discounts, and the same type of discount, such as a student discount, can be applied in multiple theaters.
