Welcome to the beating heart of our theater management system, a digital world where stories come to life and the curtain rises every night. At the center of this universe lies the very essence of dramatic art: the **Theatrical work**. Each work is a unique entity, defined by its unmistakable **Title** and the **year of creation**, which establishes its historical origin. Of course, no work would exist without its creator. For this reason, each theatrical work is inextricably linked to its **Author**, whose **Name**, **Surname** and the fundamental dates of his life, the **Date of birth** and the possible **Date of death**. A single author may be the mastermind behind numerous works, but each work in our catalog dates back to a single, specific author.

A written work, however, is just ink on paper until it finds its home: the **Theatre**. Each theater is a physical place, with its own **name**, an **address** to reach it and a **telephone** to contact it. This is where the magic happens. When an opera is staged, an **Opera on the billboard** is born, which represents the programming of that specific opera in a specific theater for a defined period, between a **Start date** and a **Final date**. A theater can host a rich program with many different works over the course of a season, and the same play can be performed in multiple productions, perhaps in different theaters or at different times.

Audience experience is key, and that includes setting ticket prices. The **Price** of a seat is not a single value, but the result of a combination of factors. First of all, it is the **Theatre** itself that establishes its own price lists. Additionally, the price varies based on **Show Category**, the **Description** of which could distinguish between a premiere, a repeat or a special event. Finally, the cost depends on the **Type of seat** chosen by the spectator, whether it is an armchair in the stalls or a seat in the gallery, each with its specific **Description**. Every single price recorded in the system is therefore the meeting point between the theater that applies it, the category of the show and the type of seat. To make culture more accessible, each theater has the right to offer discounts. These are defined as **Reduction Type**, each with a clear **description** (e.g. "students" or "groups"). The relationship is flexible: a theater can offer multiple reductions and, vice versa, the same type of reduction can be adopted by different theatres, creating a network of opportunities for spectators.
