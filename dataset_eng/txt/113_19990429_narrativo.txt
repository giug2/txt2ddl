Imagine exploring the complex and fascinating structure of an academic institution, a logically organized and interconnected universe of knowledge. This system was designed precisely to map this world, telling the story of how knowledge is organized, structured and transmitted. At the top of our organization we find the Departments, each identified by a unique code and a name that defines its general area of ​​competence, such as "Engineering" or "Letters and Philosophy". Each Department is the academic home of numerous teachers, professionals and researchers, also recognized by a personal code and name, who represent the beating heart of teaching and research.

Knowledge itself is structured hierarchically, starting from the large scientific-disciplinary sectors, which are large areas of knowledge defined by a code and a name. An interesting aspect is that a single teacher can belong to multiple sectors, demonstrating his versatility and interdisciplinary competence. Each Sector, in turn, is divided into more specific Disciplines; for example, the field of "Information Technology" may contain disciplines such as "Databases" or "Artificial Intelligence", each with its own distinctive code and name.

The Disciplines then take concrete life in the individual teaching subjects. A Subject is a specific declination of a Discipline, distinguished by a suffix (such as "Advanced" or "II") which, together with the link with the parent discipline, creates a unique identity. It is fascinating to note how subjects can be connected to each other in a preparatory relationship, creating coherent learning paths in which one subject acts as a prerequisite for another.

All this knowledge is finally delivered through the Courses. A Course, identified by its own acronym (CS), represents a complete course of study, such as a three-year or master's degree. Each Course is defined by a set of Subjects that compose it and can be divided into different Semesters to punctuate the training path. The circle closes with the teaching staff: a single teacher can be responsible or owner of multiple courses, guiding students through their training paths and thus connecting the organizational structure with the actual delivery of teaching.
