So, we basically handle a little bit of flight related stuff here. First of all, we have nations, each with its own code and name, and within each nation there are lots of cities. Obviously, each city belongs to one and only one nation. Then, cities can have multiple airports, but an airport is linked to a specific city, there is no escape. Airlines are also linked to a country, say their nationality, and a country can have many companies.

Moving on to flights, each company has quite a few flights scheduled. To be identified, a flight needs not only its number but also the company that operates it, otherwise it makes no sense. These scheduled flights have an airport where they depart from and one where they arrive; obviously, an infinite number of different flights depart and arrive from an airport. Each flight is also associated with a specific type of aircraft, such as a Boeing or an Airbus, but one aircraft model can be used for many different flights. Furthermore, flights are scheduled on certain days of the week: a flight can be active on several days and, vice versa, on a given day there are many flights.

Then there is the more concrete part. A scheduled flight is one thing, a flight that actually leaves on a certain day is another. For this reason there is a kind of register of "actual flights", where for each date it is noted what time a certain flight left and arrived. Therefore, a scheduled flight can have many real occurrences over time. Finally, we have the actual aircraft, the physical ones with their serial number. Each aircraft belongs to a certain type or model, and for each model there are many aircraft. It is one of these specific aircraft that is assigned to an actual flight on a certain date; the same plane can make a lot of flights, but a single actual flight is operated by only one plane.
