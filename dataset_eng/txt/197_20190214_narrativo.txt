Imagine running an academic institution, a vibrant place of learning and teaching. At the heart of our organizational system we find the entity of the **Course**, each single training path uniquely identified by its own specific code, the Course_ID. Each course is not an isolated entity, but is part of a well-defined structure. First of all, each course belongs to a **Department**, which groups subjects into thematic areas, such as "Humanities" or "Engineering". Each department, identified by a code, can obviously offer numerous courses, but each course belongs to a single department. To guide students in their choice, each course is associated with a precise **Level** of difficulty, described by a code and a description such as "Introductory" or "Advanced". A level can be applied to many different courses, but each course has only one reference level.

The true soul of a course is who teaches it. For this reason, each course is entrusted to a **Teacher**, identified by his ID and name. Although a teacher may be responsible for multiple courses, each individual course is taught by a single professor, ensuring teaching consistency. The teachers themselves are organized within the institution: each of them is assigned a specific **Role**, such as "Full Professor" or "Researcher", defined by a code and a description. A role can be filled by many teachers, but each teacher has only one official qualification.

Finally, to give concreteness to the teaching, each course is divided into a series of **Lessons**. Each lesson is identified not only by a progressive number, but also by the course to which it belongs, creating an indissoluble bond: a lesson exists only as a function of its course. A course, of course, is made up of many lessons. In order to take place, every single lesson requires a physical space, i.e. a **Classroom**. Each lesson is held in a specific room, identified by a code, but the same room can host countless lessons of different courses over time, thus becoming the crossroads of academic life.
