So, basically we have a few things that revolve around the Courses. Each course has its own identification code, a title and a sort of description to make it clear what it is about. These courses are not thrown out there at random, each one belongs to a single Department, which in turn is identified simply by its name. Clearly, one department can offer a lot of different courses. Each course is then linked to a specific Academic Year, which has its own code and the actual year, and takes place in a specific Room, which we recognize by a code and which has a name and a certain number of places. The same classroom, obviously, can be used for multiple courses.

A course is made up of a lot of lessons, and to avoid confusion, each lesson is set at a precise date and time, which together with the reference to the course makes it unique. The structure of a course, i.e. what is done inside, is defined by a kind of Programme. This program is practically an ordered list of pieces that make up the course. These pieces are the Teaching Modules, each with its code and title. The clever thing is that a learning module can be used in many different programs, not just one. And who makes these modules? Well, each module has its Author, who has a name and a code, and an author may have prepared quite a few modules.

Finally, let's talk about who teaches. There are the teachers, with registration number, name and surname. Their connection with the Departments is a bit complicated. On the one hand, a faculty member may be affiliated with multiple departments and, vice versa, a department is full of faculty members. On the other hand, there is a special relationship: each department has only one teacher who presides over it, who has the main chair, and a teacher can be the head of only one department. Basically there's the boss and then everyone else who works there.
