So, in practice we have these large groups, the Departments, which are recognized by their name and have an office somewhere. Each Department, in turn, manages a number of degree courses. To understand what course we are talking about, the name is not enough, you also need to know which department it is connected to; in addition, there is information on the year in which it all started. These degree courses, among other things, are grouped into a sort of broader category, a Disciplinary Area, which has its own identification code and description. Let's say that many courses can end up under the same area, but one course only fits into a specific area. Going into more detail, each Degree Course is made up of a lot of subjects, Teachings. Here too, to identify a subject, you need to know what it is called and which course it belongs to. For each course we know how many credits it is worth, the number of hours of lessons and what year of the course it is in. Finally, there are those who teach this stuff: the teachers. Each teacher has his own unique number, a kind of level or band, a certain number of years of experience and an office. This is how it works: a professor can teach several subjects, but each individual subject is taught by only one teacher.
