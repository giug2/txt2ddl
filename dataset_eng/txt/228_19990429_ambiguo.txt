So, in practice the system manages a lot of products, each identified by its own SKU code and also by the group to which it belongs, which would be a subcategory. These subcategories, which have a name and a code, are in turn grouped into larger categories, such as the main article families. So, a main category can contain quite a few different subcategories, and within each subcategory there are a lot of products.

The goods do not arrive like this, but in batches, each with its own code, quantity and the date it was made. Each product can be present in an infinite number of different batches. These lots, obviously, come from someone: the suppliers, who we recognize by their VAT number and company name. A lot of different batches can come from the same supplier, but every single batch can always be traced back to one and the same supplier. Speaking of suppliers, it seems that for every category of stuff there can be multiple suppliers dealing with it.

Then there are the warehouses, each with its own code and address. The interesting thing is that a warehouse can be the point of reference for many different suppliers. Oh, and there's something a bit peculiar about products: it seems that a product can be connected to many other products, as if one were the main piece and the others were accessories or alternative versions, a sort of internal link between them.
